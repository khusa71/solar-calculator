How the math works.

Every figure on the front page is derived, not declared. This page traces each output back to its inputs, the formula that connects them, and the assumption baked into each constant. If you disagree with a number, you should be able to find exactly which assumption to argue with.

§01

The six inputs you give.

Six inputs is the minimum for an honest answer. Anything below that is a guess; anything above that is theatre. Here's what each one drives:

InputDrives
Peak & low monthly bill (or lifestyle stamp)The 12-month bill curve, annual consumption (back-calculated from tariff)
State / DISCOMTariff (₹/unit), sun zone, net-metering settlement period, state subsidy
Roof typeMounting cost adder; tilt/orientation derate (sloped only)
Grid reliabilityOutage hours/day → battery sizing, recommended system type
System typeOn-grid / Hybrid / Off-grid — changes cost, savings model, outage value
System size (kW)Generation, cost, subsidy, and everything downstream

Advanced settings let you override panel technology, shading, cost-per-kW, tariff escalation, alt-investment rate, and analysis horizon. All have sensible defaults — they exist to let skeptics stress-test, not to be required.

§02

Modeling your bill.

Indian residential bills swing strongly with seasons — May–June peak, December–January low. Asking for 12 separate values is friction; asking for one annual figure loses the structure that makes solar pay (peak bills determine solar's break-even).

So we ask for two anchor points: peak summer and low winter. Then we interpolate across a fixed 12-month shape curve calibrated against typical Indian load patterns:

bill[m] = bill_low + (bill_peak − bill_low) × shape[m] shape = [0.00, 0.05, 0.20, 0.50, 1.00, 1.00, 0.75, 0.70, 0.50, 0.25, 0.10, 0.00] months = Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

From the bill we back-calculate annual consumption: consumption_kWh = annual_bill / tariff. We don't ask the user for kWh — most don't know it, but everyone knows their bill.

§03

Modeling generation.

One kWp of solar in India produces ~1,400–1,750 kWh/year depending on location and conditions. We model the monthly arithmetic explicitly so the user can see why July generates less than May:

generation[m] = kw × «peak sun hours» × days_in_month[m] × seasonal_factor[m] // 0.75–0.95 per month × (1 − temp_loss[m]) // hot panels lose efficiency × shading_factor // 1.00 / 0.88 / 0.75 × roof_factor // 1.00 flat / 0.93 sloped × inverter_factor // 0.97 × soiling_factor // 0.97 — dust accumulation

Peak sun hours by zone

Daily kWh per installed kW the panel would produce under standard test conditions:

ZonePSHStates
Very high5.5Rajasthan, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh
High5.0UP, Bihar, Haryana, Punjab, Telangana, Delhi
Moderate4.8Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha
Lower4.2Kerala, West Bengal, NE states, J&K, Himachal

Temperature loss

Solar panels lose efficiency when hot — counterintuitively, cool sunny days in November beat hot June afternoons. We assume panel temperature runs ~25 °C above ambient, then apply a per-degree coefficient:

temp_loss = max(0, panel_temp − 25 °C) × tech_coeff TOPCon coeff: 0.0029 / °C // newer, less heat-sensitive Mono PERC coeff: 0.0035 / °C

For Rajasthan in May (panel temp ~63 °C), the temp loss alone is ~11% on TOPCon, ~13% on Mono PERC.

Shading and roof

Shading is a single ratio applied to all months. It's the user's job to estimate it honestly — partial means an obstruction shadows ~12% of the array somewhere during the day; heavy means it's a fundamentally suboptimal site (don't build).

Roof type's main role is cost: sloped tiled roofs need extra mounting hardware (₹3,000/kW), flat RCC and terraces don't. Sloped roofs additionally take a 7% generation derate to capture typical real-world tilt/orientation losses — sloped roofs lock the panel to the roof's pitch and azimuth, which is rarely the optimal angle for the latitude.

§04

Net metering & the APPC haircut.

Generation isn't savings. Savings are the realised value of each kWh — and that value depends on what the DISCOM does with kWh you export. India has two settlement models:

Annual settlement

Most northern and southern states. Surplus units bank against deficit units across the year. At year-end:

offset_units = min(annual_gen, annual_consumption) surplus_units = max(0, annual_gen − annual_consumption) annual_savings = offset_units × tariff + surplus_units × APPC_rate // ~₹3.25/unit

So if your panels generate exactly your consumption, every kWh is worth the full retail tariff. If they generate more than you consume, the surplus is paid at the Average Power Purchase Cost — a deliberately low wholesale rate (~₹3.25/unit in 2026), not the retail tariff. That's the APPC haircut: oversize and your marginal kWh earns less than half what you'd save by consuming it.

Monthly settlement

Maharashtra, Gujarat, West Bengal. Each month nets independently. You can't bank July's surplus to offset December's bill — June surplus pays at APPC even if December needs the units. This penalises seasonal sizing: oversize for May–June and you bleed APPC every month.

monthly_savings[m] = min(gen[m], consumption[m]) × tariff + max(0, gen[m] − consumption[m]) × APPC

Without net metering

If the user disables net metering, only daytime self-consumption counts. We assume 65% of the day's load can be served live (the rest happens in evening/night when there's no sun):

monthly_savings[m] = min(gen[m], consumption[m] × 0.65) × tariff

This is the worst case for solar economics — every kWh you don't consume in real-time is wasted. Battery systems mitigate this by storing daytime surplus for evening use.

§05

Cost build-up.

The price quoted by an installer is one number. We break it apart:

gross = solar_hardware // kw × ₹65,000 default + roof_adder // kw × ₹3,000 if sloped, else 0 + battery_cost // hybrid + off-grid only + inverter_premium // hybrid +₹5k/kW; off-grid +₹15k/kW + critical_load_panel_cost // ₹20,000 flat (battery systems)

Battery sizing

Hybrid uses the larger of two rules:

  • Evening peak rule: 1 kWh battery per 1 kW solar. Covers ~3–4 hours of household evening load.
  • Outage rule: critical_load × outage_hours × 1.5 / round_trip_eff — sized to keep critical loads on through the typical outage with 50% margin.

Result is rounded up to the next 0.5 kWh. Battery cost = sized kWh × ₹35,000/kWh.

Off-grid sizes for autonomy days (default 2): battery_kwh = ceil(daily_consumption × autonomy / 0.70) — the 0.70 floor accounts for 80% depth-of-discharge ÷ 92% round-trip efficiency.

§06

Subsidies.

The PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana is the central subsidy, applicable to on-grid and hybrid systems (not off-grid). It uses a slab structure capped at 3 kW:

System sizeSubsidy band
First 2 kW₹30,000 per kW
kW 2–3₹18,000 per kW
Above 3 kWno additional subsidy

So a 3 kW system gets the maximum: 2 × 30,000 + 1 × 18,000 = ₹78,000. A 5 kW system also gets ₹78,000 — the marginal economics of the 4th and 5th kW are subsidy-free.

Some states layer additional subsidies on top. Delhi adds ₹2,000/kW (capped at ₹10,000); Gujarat adds a flat ₹10,000. Off-grid systems are excluded from PM Surya Ghar entirely.

net_cost = max(0, gross_cost − central_subsidy − state_subsidy)
§07

25-year cashflow.

Year 1 savings is one figure. The 25-year story has three time-varying lines and one recurring item:

Degradation

Panels lose ~0.5% capacity per year, floored at 85%. By year 25, generation is ~88% of year-1 output.

degradation[n] = max(0.85, 1 − 0.005 × (n − 1))

Tariff escalation

Indian residential tariffs have risen ~5% annually over the past decade. The user can override (advanced settings); 5% is the default.

escalation[n] = (1 + tariff_escalation) ^ (n − 1)

Battery replacements

LFP batteries last ~10 calendar years, or fewer if cycled deeply. We compute a replacement interval and deduct cost at every multiple within the horizon — for a 25-year horizon and a 10-year battery, that's two replacements (Y10 and Y20). Each replacement is priced at that year's battery cost, with 4%/year cost decline applied:

repl_interval = min(10 yrs, cycle_life / annual_cycles) repl_cost[year] = battery_kwh × ₹35,000 × (1 − 0.04) ^ year

For a 4 kWh battery, Y10 replacement ≈ ₹93,000; Y20 replacement ≈ ₹62,000. The first version of this calculator only modeled one replacement — a quietly significant understatement of hybrid cost. Now we deduct every replacement that falls inside the horizon.

Outage value (hybrid only)

Battery backup during outages has economic value: kWh delivered when the grid is down at a 2× premium (it's worth more than retail because nothing else can substitute). Net of round-trip efficiency losses, this becomes an annual recurring positive cashflow.

Standby drain (hybrid + off-grid)

Inverters draw ~30 W continuously (~263 kWh/year) just to stay alert for grid changes. At ₹8.50/unit that's ~₹2,235/year — a small but recurring deduction.

Putting it together

savings[n] = annual_savings_yr1 × degradation[n] × escalation[n] + outage_value − standby_drain − battery_replacement_cost (when applicable) cumulative[n] = cumulative[n−1] + savings[n] // initialised at −net_cost
§08

IRR, payback, net gain.

Simple payback

The first year where cumulative cashflow turns positive, with linear interpolation within the year for fractional precision.

Discounted payback

Same idea, but each year's savings discounted at 6% before accumulation. Always longer than simple payback. We compute it but don't headline it — most users find simple payback more intuitive.

IRR

The discount rate that makes the net present value of the 25-year cashflow stream equal zero. Solved by binary search over [−99%, 500%]:

find r such that: Σ (cashflow[t] / (1 + r)^t) = 0 over t = 0..25 where cashflow[0] = −net_cost

IRR is the right comparison metric for solar because it accounts for time value of money. A 7-year payback at high IRR can beat a 5-year payback at low IRR if the late-year cashflows are larger (they are, due to tariff escalation).

Net gain

Total savings over 25 years minus the upfront net cost. This is the cleanest "what's in your pocket at year 25" figure — same units as the alt-investment comparisons.

net_gain = Σ savings[n] − net_cost
§09

Comparisons against FD & equity.

"Should I go solar?" reduces to "vs what?" The honest comparison is: take the same outlay and put it in an alternative investment, then live in the same house and pay your bills out of pocket. The lowest-friction alternatives for an Indian household are fixed deposits and equity index funds.

The mistake we used to make

The first version of the chart compared raw investment growth against solar's net gain. That made alts look enormous: an FD line said ₹16 lakh, equity said ₹50 lakh, while solar showed ₹18 lakh. Apparent landslide for equity.

But the FD investor still pays bills for 25 years. The equity investor still pays bills for 25 years. Solar's "savings" are those same bills, avoided. So if we don't subtract bills paid from the alt rows, we're double-counting in solar's favour by every avoided bill — and the visual is dishonest in equity's favour by every bill they actually had to pay.

What we do now

Each alt row is rendered as net wealth at horizon: post-tax investment gain, minus cumulative bills paid (which escalate at the same tariff rate the solar savings escalate at). Solar's net gain is unchanged — it already nets all of this out.

bills_total = annual_bill × ((1+esc)^H − 1) / esc fd_wealth = (fd_corpus_post − net_cost) − bills_total equity_wealth = (equity_corpus_post − net_cost) − bills_total solar_wealth = net_gain // already net of outlay and avoided bills

Tax treatment

AlternativeTax basisEffective net rate
Fixed Deposit20% slab tax on interest~5.6% from 7% gross
Equity index fund12.5% LTCG on gains~10.7% from 12% gross

The slab assumption (20%) is a typical middle-class income-tax bracket. For higher earners FD looks worse; for lower it looks slightly better. We don't ask for your tax bracket — defaults serve 80% of users.

Why FD often goes negative

A 7% FD post-tax (~5.6%) compounds slower than tariff escalation (~5%) plus household consumption inflation. Over 25 years, the FD investor's gain barely covers the bills they had to pay — often it's less than the bills, so net wealth is negative. The Card A row will show this honestly.

§10

The verdict logic.

The single sentence at the top of the page is computed, not authored. It compares solar's net wealth to passive equity's net wealth (the fairest financial benchmark — what a passive index investor would gain over the same 25 years):

Gap (solar − equity)Verdict
> +₹2 lakhSolar wins clearly
−₹1 lakh to +₹2 lakhComparable to equity (choose for inflation hedge + energy security)
< −₹1 lakh, but solar > 0Equity wins on cash, but solar still gains and adds outage cover
solar < 0Wrong system at this outlay

This logic deliberately doesn't depend on IRR alone. A 14% IRR can lose to a 12% equity alt when the outlay is large enough that absolute wealth gains diverge — typical for hybrid systems above 4 kW. IRR is shown as part of the verdict body but doesn't drive the headline.

Why this matters

The earlier verdict said "Solar wins clearly — 18% return beats your 12% alternative" while the chart's endpoints showed solar at ₹18 lakh and equity at ₹58 lakh. Both lines were technically computing different things; the user couldn't tell. Aligning verdict and chart on the same wealth metric is the single biggest honesty improvement in this version.

§12

What we deliberately don't model.

An honest tool says what it leaves out. None of the items below are oversights — each is a deliberate choice to prefer simplicity over precision when the precision wouldn't change the decision.

  • Inverter replacement. String inverters often need replacement around year 12 (~₹5,500/kW in 2026 prices). Knocks IRR by ~1.5pp. Currently not deducted; will be added in v1.1.
  • Loan financing. Many users finance solar through PM Surya Ghar's collateral-free ₹2 lakh loan at 6.75%. We assume cash purchase. With a loan, payback is faster by EMI logic but total cost is slightly higher.
  • Slab tariff. Some DISCOMs charge higher rates above a consumption threshold (e.g., ₹6/unit below 200 units, ₹8/unit above). We use a single weighted average tariff per state. For users near a slab boundary this can under-state savings by 5–10%.
  • AMC costs. Annual maintenance contracts run ₹500–1,500/year per kW after the warranty period. Currently not deducted.
  • Tilt and azimuth optimisation. Optimal tilt for India is latitude-dependent. We assume optimal mounting on flat roofs and apply a 7% derate for sloped roofs (see §03).
  • Time-of-day tariffs. A few DISCOMs are piloting time-of-use rates that would change battery economics. Not yet modeled.
  • Resale value of the solar system. A 5-year-old system has economic value; we treat it as zero (conservative).
  • Income tax deduction on solar (Section 80EEB-style). Not currently available for residential solar in India. If introduced, IRR would improve.
  • Carbon credits. Residential solar in India isn't currently monetised through carbon markets. We show CO₂ offset as an environmental figure only.
